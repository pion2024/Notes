# chap 1 : finite linear representation of finite groups 

- Def (Group Representation) : Given a group $G$ and a field $\mathbb{F}$. A representation of $G$ is a pair $(V, \rho)$, where $V$ is a vector space over $\mathbb{F}$, and $\rho: G \to \text{GL}(V)$ is a group homomorphism. . 
  - basic properties : for any $g_1, g_2 \in G$ and $v \in V$, we have $\rho(g_1 g_2)(v) = \rho(g_1)(\rho(g_2)(v))$,  $\rho(e)(v) = v$  and $\rho(s^{-1})(v) = (\rho(s))^{-1}v$
- notations : 
  - we also write $\rho_s$ for $\rho(s)$
  - by abuse of language, we sometimes say that $V$ is a representation space of $G$ when $\rho$ is clear. 
  - degree of a representation means the dimension of the representation space 
- one is <u>interested in dealing with a finite number of elements $x_i$ of $V$</u> for most applications and can always find a finite dimensional subrepresentation (space) of $V$ that contains $x_i$ by considering the vector subspace generated by $\rho_s(x_i)$.
  - $G$ is finite and the number of $x_i$ is finite so the number of $\rho_s(x_i)$ is finite 
- examples
  - using the finite property : consider a finite group G and its linear representation of degree 1 i.e. a homomorphism from G to the multiplicative group $C^*$, then the images $\rho(s)$ are roots of the unity, following the Lagrange theorem, but may not contain all the g-th roots. For example when $\rho$ is the trivial representation there is only one g-th root. related : homomorphism theorem.
  - trivial rep that maps all group element to the $id$
  - **regular rep** $\rho_{reg}$ : consider a vector space $V$ with bases indexed by elements of $G$: $(e_t)_{t\in G}$. the regular rep is defined as $\rho_{reg}(s)e_t = e_{st} $

## $G$-homomorphism

- def ($G$-homomorphism and Isomorphism) :
  Let $(V_1, \rho_1)$ and $(V_2, \rho_2)$ be two representations of $G$. If there exists an $\mathbb{F}$-linear map $\phi: V_1 \to V_2$ such that for any $g \in G$ and $v \in V_1$, the following commutativity condition holds:

  $$\phi(\rho_1(g)(v)) = \rho_2(g)(\phi(v))$$

  then $\phi$ is called a $G$-homomorphism. ( Morphism in $\text{Rep}_{\mathbb{F}}(G)$)

- If the $G$-homomorphism $\phi$ is also a bijection (i.e., it is a **linear isomorphism**), then $\phi$ is called a $G$-isomorphism, denoted as $(V_1, \rho_1) \cong (V_2, \rho_2)$. In this case, $\phi^{-1}: V_2 \to V_1$ is also a $G$-isomorphism.

  - as a consequence if $(V_1, \rho_1) \cong (V_2, \rho_2)$ then $V_1 \cong V_2$ linearly. 


- examples of $G$-homomorphism :

  -  if $\rho_2$ is the trivial representation and $\rho_1$ is the regular representation on $V_1$ whose basis are indexed by elements of $G$, then we can simply take $\phi : e_i \to 1$. and verify that $\phi$ is a $G$-homomorphism but not invertible. 

  -  matrix-free definition for $\text{Tr}$ : let $(V,\rho)$ be a representation of $\dim(n) > 1 $ and $(V^*,\rho^*)$ the representation defined on the dual space :
      $$\forall f \in V^*, \forall v \in V, \quad[\rho^*(g)(f)](v) = f(\rho(g^{-1})(v))$$. 

     Define the representation for the tensor product space $(V\otimes V^*,\rho)$:  $$\rho_{\otimes}(g)(v \otimes f) = \rho(g)(v) \otimes \rho^*(g)(f)$$

     Define $(V_1, \rho_1)$ as $(V \otimes V^*, \rho_{\otimes})$ . it's a representation of dimension $n^2$.

     Define $(V_2, \rho_2)$ : the trivial representation over $\mathbb{F}$ :  $\rho_2(g) = \text{id}$

     Define $\phi$ : $V \otimes V^* \to \mathbb{F}$ for all pure tensor and extend it linearly to $V \otimes V^*$ :  $\forall v\otimes f\in V_1, \phi(v \otimes f) = f(v)$

     We can verify that $\phi$ is a G-homomorphism and $\forall v$ since $V\otimes V^*$ is of dimension $n^2$ and isomorphic to $\text{End}(V)$, each tensor $v\otimes f = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n v^i e_i \right) \otimes \left(\sum_{j=1}^n f^je^j\right)$ can be represented by a matrix $$A = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n A_{ij} (e_i \otimes e^j)$$ where $A_{ij} = v^if^j$

     the reciproque is also valid i.e. given a matrix we can find $\{v^i,f^j\}_{i,j}$ such that $Aij=vifj$

     Let $\{e^j\}$ be the dual basis of $\{e^i\}$ then $$\phi(A) = \sum_{i,j} A_{ij} \delta_i^j = \sum_{i} A_{ii} = \text{Tr}(A)$$ 

### Category $\text{Rep}_{\mathbb{F}}(G)$ 

- Summary :the $G$-homomorphism is so called since we view the representations as objects in the representations spaces, instead of functions. 

- Define **Object** as representation of $G$ on $V$ i.e. element of $\{(V,\rho) | \rho : G\to \text{GL}(V) \text{ is a representation for $G$}\}$ and **Morphism** as $G$-homomorphism between two representations

- Define the 4 data structures : 

  - $\text{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$ as the class of objects 
  - $\text{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A, B)$ as the set of morphisms from object $A$ to object $B$
  - Composition as composition of function 
  - Identity maps : $\text{id}_X \in \text{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X, X)$

- We should verify the 2 axioms of category :

  - closure of compositions (composition of morphisms is still morphism) : let $\phi: V_1 \to V_2$ and $\psi: V_2 \to V_3$ be two G-homomorphisms, verify that $\psi \circ \phi : V_1 \to V_3$ is also a $G$-homomorphism :

    $$\begin{aligned} (\psi \circ \phi)(\rho_1(g)(v)) &= \psi(\phi(\rho_1(g)(v))) \\ &= \psi(\rho_2(g)(\phi(v))) \quad (\text{since } \phi \text{ is } G\text{-homo}) \\ &= \rho_3(g)(\psi(\phi(v))) \quad (\text{since } \psi \text{ is } G\text{-homo}) \\ &= \rho_3(g)((\psi \circ \phi)(v)) \end{aligned}$$

  - Associativity of morphisms
    Let $(V_1, \rho_1), (V_2, \rho_2), (V_3, \rho_3), (V_4, \rho_4)$ be representations of a group $G$.
    For $G$-homomorphisms $\phi: V_1 \to V_2$, $\psi: V_2 \to V_3$, and $\tau: V_3 \to V_4$, the composition is order-independent: $\tau \circ (\psi \circ \phi) = (\tau \circ \psi) \circ \phi$ : 

    Let $v \in V_1$, verify the associativity of the underlying maps:
    $$\begin{aligned} 
    (\tau \circ (\psi \circ \phi))(v) &= \tau((\psi \circ \phi)(v))  \\ 
    &= \tau(\psi(\phi(v)))  \\ 
    &= (\tau \circ \psi)(\phi(v))  \\ 
    &= ((\tau \circ \psi) \circ \phi)(v) 
    \end{aligned}$$

    Since the underlying maps are standard functions, they are inherently associative, meaning $(\tau \circ (\psi \circ \phi))(v) = ((\tau \circ \psi) \circ \phi)(v)$ for all $v \in V_1$. 

    Furthermore, by the closure of composition proved earlier, $\psi \circ \phi$ and $\tau \circ \psi$ are both $G$-homomorphisms. Therefore, both $\tau \circ (\psi \circ \phi)$ and $(\tau \circ \psi) \circ \phi$ are valid, identical $G$-homomorphisms from $V_1$ to $V_4$, confirming associativity of morphisms.

- the morphism $\phi$ commutes with the internal structure of object : $$\begin{array}{ccc}
  V_1 & \xrightarrow{\phi} & V_2 \\
  \downarrow{\rho_1(g)} & & \downarrow{\rho_2(g)} \\
  V_1 & \xrightarrow{\phi} & V_2
  \end{array}$$

## Subrepresentation 

- def (**subrepresentation** and embedding) : 
  Let $(W, \rho_W)$ and $(V, \rho_V)$ be representations of $G$. $(W, \rho_W)$ is said to be a subrepresentation of $(V, \rho_V)$ (or $W$ is said to be embeddable/induced **into** $V$) if and only if there exists an **injective** $G$-homomorphism $\iota: W \hookrightarrow V$.
  - if $W$ is a subrepresentation defined by the current definition, then $\iota(W) \subset V$ is a $\rho_V(G)$-invariant subspace of $V$, and $(W, \rho_W)$ is isomorphic to $(\iota(W), \rho_V|_{\iota(W)})$ which is itself a subrepresentation of $(V,\rho_V)$
  - This definition explicits that $W$ doesn't need to be inside $V$.
  - if $W \leq V$ a $\rho_V(G)$-invariant subspace of $V$, then the identity map serves as the injective $G$-homomorphism and $W$ is of course a subrepresentation of $V$. 

## Isomorphic representations 

- def : two representations $\rho: G\to \text{GL}(V)$ and $\rho': G\to \text{GL}(V')$ of the same group $G$ is said to be **isomorphic** if there exists a **$G$-isomorphism** (Intertwining operator) $\tau : V \to V'$ such that $\forall s\in G, \tau \circ \rho(s) = \rho'(s)\circ \tau $ 
- In matric form, representations $\rho $ and $\rho'$ are isomorphic i.e. there exists a invertible matrix $T$ s.t . $TR_s = R_s'T $ or $R_s = T^{-1} R_s'T $  i.e. $R_s$ similar to $R'_s$

### Maschke's Theorem :

- Thm : all subspace $W\subset V$ has a complement $W^0$ in $V$ which is stable under $G$
  - $W$ does not need to be stable, although with this condition the proof is trivial, since by Weyl's trick we can construct an $G$-invariant inner product thus $W^\perp$ is also stable. 

- lemma : let $W$ be a subspace of $V$, we can establish a bijective correspondence between the projections of $V$ onto $W$  (in general, not necessarily orthogonal) and the complements of $W$ in $V$. (alternatively, each complement corresponds to a projection)
  - the projection vanishes on $W^0$ 
- general proof of the thm: 
  take an arbitrary complement $W'$ of $W$ ,we consider the associated projection $P$ of $V$ onto $W$ whose kernel is $W'$. 
  Define $P^0 = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{t\in G} \rho_t\cdot P \cdot \rho_t^{-1}$ (average of the conjugate action of $G$ on $p$) , 
  By direct calculation we have 
  $\forall v \in V, P^0(v) \in W$ 
  and
   $\forall x\in W, P^0(x) = x$ 
  so $P^0$ is a projection of $V$ onto $W$, corresponding to some complement $W^0$ of $W$, and $W^0 = \text{Ker}(P^0)$. 
  It suffices to prove that $W^0$ is stable under $G$ 
  Note that for all $s\in G$, $P^0$ commutes with $\rho_s$ i.e. $\rho_s \circ P^0 = P^0 \circ \rho_s$ (multiplication by $\rho_s$ permutes the index) and $\forall x\in W, P^0(x) = x $, thus 
  $\forall s \in G, \forall x \in W^0, P^0\circ \rho_s(x) = \rho_s\circ P^0(x) = 0 \implies P^0(\rho_s(x)) = 0 \implies \rho_s(x) \in W^0 $   Q.E.D

## Tensor product 

- **Tensor product of vector spaces**  : the tensor product of two vector spaces $V_1$ and $V_2$ is defined as the pair $(T,\tau)$ consisting of a vector space $T$ and a **bilinear** map $\tau \colon V_1 \times V_2 \to T$, which satisfies the following properties: 

  - For all $\{u_i\}$ basis of $V_1$ and all $\{v_j\}$ basis of $V_2$,  $\{\tau(u_i,v_j)\}$ is a basis of $T$.

- (Basis-free definition) : Let $V_1$ and $V_2$ be vector spaces. A tensor product of $V_1$ and $V_2$ is a pair $(T, \tau)$, consisting of a vector space $T$ and a bilinear map $\tau \colon V_1 \times V_2 \to T$, which satisfies the following **universal property**: 

  - For every vector space $U$ and every bilinear map $f \colon V_1 \times V_2 \to U$, there **exists** a **unique** (up to isomorphism) linear map $L \colon T \to U$ such that $f = L \circ \tau$ i.e. the diagram commutes : 

  - $$
    \begin{array}{ccc}
    V_1 \times V_2 & \xrightarrow{\quad \tau \quad} & T \\
    & \searrow_{\! f} & \downarrow {\scriptstyle \exists! L} \\
    & & U
    \end{array}
    $$

  - with existence and uniqueness of $T$ , we can note $T$ as $V_1 \otimes V_2 $

- Note : 

  - Not all element $x \in V_1 \otimes V_2$ can be written as $x_1 \otimes x_2 $ where $x_1 \in V_1 \and x_2 \in V_2$ (pure tensor). For example, if the two basis are $\{e_1,e_2\}, \{f_1,f_2\}$ then $x = e_1\otimes f_1 + e_2 \otimes f_2$ can't be written as $x_1 \otimes x_2$. 
    - Proof: suppose the otherwise and proof that the system of equations involving the coefficients of $x_1$ and $x_2$ has no solution.
    - in quantum physique, $x \in V_1 \otimes V_2$ that can not be written as a tensor product is called an **Entangled State**, contrary to the **Separable State** where $\exists x_1\in V_1, \exists x_2\in V_2, x = x_1 \otimes x_2$

  - The decomposition into pure tensors combination may not be unique. So one should be careful when extending a function defined by $\phi(v \otimes w) = f(v, w)$ from a function only defined for pure tensors to all linear combinations of pure tensors in $V\otimes V$. 
    - for example, let $T = e_1 \otimes e_1 + e_1 \otimes e_2 + e_2 \otimes e_1 + e_2 \otimes e_2$ , from the distributivity we know that $T = e_1 \otimes (e_1 + e_2) + e_2 \otimes (e_1+e_2) $ so the sum of 2 pure tensors, but it is also equal to $(e_1 + e_2) \otimes (e_1 + e_2)$ so 1 pure tensor.

## Schur's lemma 

- In the following context, we have the following definition of the intertwinning operators space (morphisms space) : 
  For two representations $(V,\rho), (W,\rho')$, define $\text{Hom}_G(V, W)) \coloneqq \{f \in \text{End}(V,W) \, \mid \forall s\in G, f\rho_s = \rho'_sf\}$
- By $V \cong W$ we mean that representations $\rho$ and $\rho$ are $G$-isomorphic 
- Thm (Schur's lemma) : Given two **irreducible** representations $V,W$ of a finite group $G$, we have 
  $\dim(\text{Hom}_G(V, W)) = 1$ if $V \cong W$ and
  $\dim(\text{Hom}_G(V, W)) = 0$ if $V \not\cong W$

## <u>Schur's Orthogonality Relations</u>

- Title underlined means these are only notes for the first lecture, I gave it a quick read to get the general idea.

- Thm : the characters of irreducible representations form an orthogonal basis i.e. 
  $\langle \chi_{V_i}, \chi_{V_j} \rangle_G = \delta_{ij}$

- Thm : Let $X,Y$ be (the spaces of ) two representations of a finite group $G$, then $\dim(\text{Hom}_G(X, Y)) = \langle \chi_X, \chi_Y \rangle_G$

  - By Maschke's theorem, $X,Y$ can be decomposed into direct sums of irreducible representations :  $$X \cong \bigoplus_{i=1}^k a_i V_i, \quad Y \cong \bigoplus_{i=1}^k b_i V_i$$

    <u>The $\text{Hom}$ functor is bilinear</u>, so
     $$\begin{aligned}
    \text{Hom}_G(X, Y) &\cong \text{Hom}_G\left(\bigoplus_{i} a_i V_i, \bigoplus_{j} b_j V_j\right) \\
    &\cong \bigoplus_{i, j} \text{Hom}_G(a_i V_i, b_j V_j) \\
    &\cong \bigoplus_{i, j} \big( a_i b_j \text{Hom}_G(V_i, V_j) \big)
    \end{aligned}$$

    $$\dim(\text{Hom}_G(X, Y)) = \sum_{i, j} a_i b_j \dim(\text{Hom}_G(V_i, V_j))$$ 

    By Schur's lemma, $\dim(\text{Hom}_G(V_i, V_j))$ equals 0 if $V_i \ncong V_j$ else 1, so 

    $$\dim(\text{Hom}_G(X, Y)) = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i b_i$$

    Since $$\chi_X = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i \chi_{V_i}, \quad \chi_Y = \sum_{i=1}^k b_i \chi_{V_i}$$

    so $$\begin{aligned}
    \langle \chi_X, \chi_Y \rangle_G &= \left\langle \sum_{i} a_i \chi_{V_i}, \sum_{j} b_j \chi_{V_j} \right\rangle_G \\
    &= \sum_{i, j} a_i b_j \langle \chi_{V_i}, \chi_{V_j} \rangle_G
    \end{aligned}$$

    By Schur's orthogonality, we know that for irreducible representations $\langle \chi_{V_i}, \chi_{V_j} \rangle_G = \delta_{ij}$

    thus $$\langle \chi_X, \chi_Y \rangle_G = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i b_i = \dim(\text{Hom}_G(X, Y))$$

## criterion of Induced representation

- Notes :

  -  given a representation $\rho$, we can always write $\sum_{r \in R}\rho_rW$ , but not $\bigoplus_{r\in R}\rho_rW$ before verifying that the sum is direct. It would be better to always proceed in two steps (sum eq V and sum direct) in the verification of whether $(V,\rho)$ is the induced representation of $(W,\theta)$
  - (c.f. to the existence and uniqueness theorem below) to show that an induced representation $(V,\rho)$ does exist, it suffices to show that the current representation $(W,\theta)$ can be embedded into another representation i.e. it is (isomorphic to) a subrepresentation $(W',\theta')$ of an existing representation $(V',\rho')$ and use the fact that subrepresentation can be induced to a subrepresentation (induction preserves subrep)

- I thought that by "induced", we mean the construction of a representation for the bigger group $G$. What's the point if we already know that $G$ already has a representation $\rho$ ?

  - While the definition here is called "induced representation", the word "induced" means rather that we verify if a given representation for the bigger group is induced by a subrepresentation (internal definition).

- Motivation for the definition : why the definition is of the form $\bigoplus_{r \in R} \rho(r)W = V$?

  - it's necessary that $\bigoplus_{r \in R} \rho(r)W \subseteq V$, since by a "representation induced by $\theta$ ", what we want is a representation $(V,\rho)$ of $G$ that is equal to $\theta$ on the subset $H$, so $W$ should also be stable under $\rho(H)$. Let $R$ be the system of representative, using all the hypothesis on $\rho$, we have $\forall s = rt \in G, \rho(s)W=\rho(r)\rho(t)H = \rho(r)H \subset V$, so $\bigoplus_{r \in R} \rho(r)W \subset V$. 
  - <u>the other inclusion may be explained by the fact that we want the minimal representation space .</u> And that it's dual to the Frobnius Reciprocity 

- Methods to construct the induction : 

  - Function space method（physic, Serre Ex3.5)  : Instead of constructing the tuples with external direct sum, we construct the functions that are defined on $G$ and take value in $W$ i.e. $f : G\to W$  that satisfy $\rho_H(h)f(t) = f(h^{-1}t) $. That means, if we already know $f(r_i)$, then $f$ is determined on the entire coset, and the action of $G$ is only to translate the variable i.e. $(s \cdot f)(t) = f(s^{-1}t)$. This method is useful in the lie representation. 
  - Tensor product method : using Group Algebra $\mathbb{C}[G]$ and $\mathbb{C}[H]$. $$V = \mathbb{C}[G] \otimes_{\mathbb{C}[H]} W$$. $\mathbb{C}[G]$ offers the element $s \otimes w$ 

- Be cautious of the notations : $\theta$ is the restriction of representation $\rho_H$ restricted on the subspace $W$ , i.e. $\theta = \rho_H|_W$ that's why we use $\theta$ instead of simple $\rho_H$ . Their matrices are of different dimensions.

## Existence and uniqueness of induced representation 

- ![image-20260407185639029](C:\Users\T480s\AppData\Roaming\Typora\typora-user-images\image-20260407185639029.png)
- existence : embed $W$ into the regular representation (consider $\theta$ as irrep so it's isomorphic to a sub regular representation) $G$, hence it can be induced into $V$. 
- uniqueness up to an **G-isomorphism**: 
  - let $f: W \hookrightarrow V'$ be the injection from $W$ to $V'$ such that $f(\omega) = \omega$ since $V,V'$ are both induced by $W$. 
  - $W$ is a $H$-invariant space of $V$​, so  $\forall t \in H, f(\theta_t w) = \theta_t w = \rho'_t w = \rho'_t f(w)$. This satistifes the condition of Lemma 1 .  By lemma 1 we know that $f$ can be extended to $F$ that satisfies $\forall s \in G, \forall x \in V, F(\rho_sx)=\rho'_sF(x)$ and $F|_W = f$ so $F$ is a $G$-homomorphism
  - $V,V'$ being induced by $W$, their dimensions are the same $$\dim(V) = [G:H] \cdot \dim(W) = \dim(V')$$. So it suffices to prove that $F$ is surjective : trivial because $\forall y\in V', \exists s\in G,s.t.y =  \rho'_s\omega  = \rho'_sF(\omega) = F(\rho_s\omega)$. so $F$ is $G$-isomorphism
